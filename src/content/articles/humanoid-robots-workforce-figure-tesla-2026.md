For most of the past decade, humanoid robotics was the domain of impressive demos that couldn't survive contact with the real world. Robots that could do backflips on stage but couldn't open a door reliably. That era is over.

## What's Actually Working, Right Now

Figure 02 robots are performing productive assembly work at BMW's Spartanburg, South Carolina plant. Tesla's Optimus Gen 3 units are working in Tesla's own factories, handling parts logistics and basic assembly tasks. Neither deployment is fully autonomous — human supervisors remain close — but both represent genuine, economically productive work by general-purpose humanoid robots.

The shift from demo to production happened faster than almost anyone predicted, driven primarily by the dramatic improvement in embodied AI models — systems that translate natural language instructions and visual perception into physical actions.

## The Technology That Made It Possible

**Vision-language-action models**: The same transformer architecture behind GPT and image generation has been adapted for robotics. Models like Google's RT-2, Physical Intelligence's π0, and Figure's own embodied AI translate what the robot sees and hears into precise motor commands.

**Sim-to-real transfer**: Generating synthetic training data in simulation has accelerated the development cycle by an order of magnitude. A robot can "practice" millions of grasping variations in simulation before touching a single physical object.

**Dexterous hands**: The manipulation of objects with human-like dexterity — long the most challenging hardware problem — has made significant advances. Figure 02's hands can manipulate objects as small as a USB connector with 95%+ reliability.

## The Economic Arithmetic

Goldman Sachs estimates the total addressable market for humanoid robots at $38 trillion by 2035 — a number that sounds absurd until you consider that global labor costs are approximately $50 trillion annually, and a humanoid robot operating at human-level capability would cost a fraction of a human worker over a multi-year deployment.

At current production scales, Figure 02 units cost approximately $35,000. Tesla projects Optimus will reach $20,000 within five years of mass production. At those price points, the economics for physically demanding, repetitive tasks are compelling even with current capability limitations.

## The Labor Question Nobody Wants to Answer

The cheerful framing from robotics companies is that humanoids will take over dangerous, dirty, and dull tasks, freeing humans for more meaningful work. History suggests this framing is incomplete.

The first wave of industrial automation created enormous wealth and eventually expanded employment — but the transition period lasted decades and inflicted significant economic hardship on specific communities that never fully recovered. The pace of humanoid robotics deployment is orders of magnitude faster than the first industrial revolution.

Policy frameworks, retraining programs, and social safety nets designed for that slower transition are not adequate for what's coming.

## The Next 24 Months

Every major robotics company has announced aggressive production ramp targets for 2026 and 2027. Figure targets 1,000 robots deployed by end of 2026. Tesla has hinted at 10,000+ Optimus units in 2026. Boston Dynamics' Atlas commercial program is accelerating. Agility Robotics' Digit is expanding beyond Amazon warehouses.

The robots are coming to work. The question is whether we're building the institutions to manage that transition before the wave arrives.
